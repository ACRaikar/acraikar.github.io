EE 701 - Internet of Things

Internet of Things is the buzz phrase that has been around for a while and will continue to be for some time. So what is this? Why do people make such a big fuss over IoT? The “Thing” in the IoT can be anything from an image sensor, to an irrigation system, to a cargo container having a temperature sensor that sends the data to the logistics coordinator. Anything that senses its surrounding environment and can communicate over the internet is called a Thing and the Internet of Things is a network, where these Things can connect as well as exchange data. A study predicts that by 2025, there will be 41.6 billion IoT devices worldwide!

Internet of Things instructed by Dr. Guan was a unique course that I took in my graduate program. It is unique because, in tandem with regular classes, there were lab sessions and literature review assignments. The course was specialized for final semester students to have an overall educational experience. Through the classes, Dr. Guan gave us an introduction to three significant Low Power Wide Area Network (LPWAN) technologies which were LoRa, Sigfox, and NB-IoT. Lora is a non-cellular modulation technology used by LoRaWAN. Sigfox is a proprietary owned network operator that builds wireless networks to connect low-power objects transmitting small amounts of data. NB-IoT is a 3GPP initiative developed to enable a wide range of new IoT devices and services. The objective of NB-IoT, which is a cellular standard, is to standardize IoT devices so that they are stable and interoperable. All of these technologies helped me to have a pragmatic view of IoT.

Dr. Guan is very amicable and instructs topics in an intuitive approach. Some aspects of this course can be traced back to EE 534 instructed by Dr. Guan himself and the fundaments of EE 534 helped me to have a better outlook of EE 701. The classes were usually non-rigid with active discussions. Professor would intrigue us with questions and acknowledge our doubts. The assignments were not usual writing or solving problems. They were paper reading assignments where we did a literature review of interesting IEEE research papers and presented in class. Many such differentiating factors made this course stand out from other coursework.

For the project in EE 701, we were given options to build an independent project or else to work in Wireless Networking and Systems (WINGS) lab and assist the Ph.D. students in their research. Given the choice, I opted for the latter and had an opportunity to work on the srsRAN project where we tried to implement a 4G LTE network on top of an Unmanned Arial Vehicle (UAV). The main goal of this project was to deploy our LTE network over multiple UAVs to establish cellular communication in remote areas, where it is hard to implement physical base stations. The project demanded a thorough understanding of the 4G LTE network which gave us practical exposure to cellular networks. We established wireless communication between eNodeB and EPC and made configurations to connect multiple eNBs to a single EPC. EE 701 provided us a fun way to explore the Internet of Things. The Professor and Teaching Assistant guided us throughout the course on having practical knowledge of the coursework. We had seminars by experts and researchers who gave us a good exposure to both industry expectation and research opportunities. Additionally, fascinating experiments were carried out in class utilizing USRP and the GNU Radio Companion program where we developed modules for absolute function and square function. Needless to say, this was an interesting course where we learned concepts, implemented them in the labs, and also took part in research projects.
